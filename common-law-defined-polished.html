Common Law Defined — Polished & Hyperlinked

“American patriots fought for the rights of Englishmen… and for the rights of man as they understood them to be guaranteed by Nature and Nature’s God.”
The Spirit of ‘Seventy-Six, p. viii

The common law of England — rooted in the laws of God, antiquity, reason, and Nature’s God — was the foundation colonists carried to America.

True Historical Definition (1527–1776)

  • John Rastell (1527): Common law = law of England alone, pre-statutory, unchanged by Parliament
  • Michael Dalton (1661): “receiving principally their grounds from the Laws of God and Nature…”
  • Sir Edward Coke (1642): Magna Carta declared “the Great Charter as the Common Law” — binding on all judges forever

Rejection of Roman Civil Law

George Leapingwell (1859): Civil law never adopted in England — King Stephen banned its study

“Stare Decisis” — A 19th-Century Fiction

No 17th/18th-century writer uses the phrase. Coined ~1820s to give judge-made law the appearance of antiquity.

Infiltration Timeline

  • 1571 — First civil-law procedure allowed in privileged courts
  • 1848 — David Dudley Field’s “Field Code” spreads Roman code-pleading across U.S.
  • 1873 — Judicature Act fuses law & equity in England — common-law supremacy ends

Conclusion

The common law the Founders knew was fixed, God-given, tied to Magna Carta — never judge-made, never statute-altered. Its deliberate corruption by civil-law codes and “stare decisis” mythology is one of history’s greatest deceptions.
