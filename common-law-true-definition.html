Historical Context: Defining the Common Law
The common law of England, as understood by English-Americans, was a distinct body of law, separate from foreign systems like Roman civil law or ecclesiastical canon law. John Rastell’s Les Termes de la Ley (originally written 1527) (book Pg.72, digital Pg. 162) articulates this clarity: “The Lawes of this Realme..], common law being one of the bodies of lawes of THAT realme, England, without any other realme” This definition, echoed in the 1721 edition, (Pg. 147 ) emphasizes its exclusivity: “Thirdly, and most usually by the Common Law is understood such Lawes as were generally taken and HELDEN for Law, BEFORE ANY STATUTE WAS MADE to alter the same”. Michael Dalton’s The Countrey Justice (1661) (book Pg. 1, digital Pg. 11) further roots it in divine and natural origins: “The Common Law of this Realm of England, receiving principally their grounds from the Laws of God and Nature… being FOR THEIR ANTIQUITY those whereby this Realm was governed many hundred years before the Conquest” .
These texts, influenced by Fortescue’s earlier works ( late 15th cen.), establish the common law as a pre-statutory, unburdened by stare decisis or judicial innovation, judge-independent system, and free of Roman civil law’s sway.

"Stare decisis," or stare decisis et non quieta movere—"You have decided to stand still and not move"—has no basis in common law. It is a phony maxim, phrased in Latin in the 19th century to evoke the appearance of antiquity, but it is nothing more than "judge-made law." I have found no specific 17th- or 18th-century document using the phrase "stare decisis." The evidence suggests the phrase was coined and gained prominence in the early 19th century, with Kent's Commentaries (1826–1830) being a key early reference, as cited in Bouvier's Law Dictionary. The absence of the phrase in key 18th-century texts, such as Blackstone’s, indicates it was not part of legal discourse during that period.

Conclusion: A Timeless Standard
The common law of England, as defined from 1527 to 1776 and embraced by colonial Americans, was a fixed, God-given system tied to Magna Carta, never a judge-made or statute-altered construct. Its rejection of Roman civil law and preservation in colonial declarations underscore its role as a bulwark of liberty. From Penn’s defense to the Seventh Amendment, it remains a testament to a legal heritage that prioritizes reason, antiquity, and the rights of Englishmen—unalterable by king or court.
